Chapter Title Cards: Design Directions

Three approaches for the cards that open each chapter. All 12 chapters are previewable within each direction. These define the visual tone for the entire series, so pick the one that feels most like the show.

Direction A: Editorial Minimalist

The Ken Burns approach taken literally.

Centered type on a dark field. Nothing else. The title does all the work, and the restraint signals confidence. This is the direction already sketched in the existing style mockups, refined here with tighter animation timing and considered negative space.

The logic: if you're making a documentary about a strategy game, the most powerful visual statement is to step away from the game entirely. The card says "this is serious" and then the gameplay footage returns with more impact because of the contrast.

This direction is the easiest to produce (static PNG export or a simple Fusion comp) and the hardest to get wrong. The risk is that it reads as too plain or too "PowerPoint" if the typography isn't dialed in exactly right.

Design Notes

Typography hierarchy: The chapter number label in Poppins uppercase reads as a structural marker ("you are here"), while the title in Lora serif carries the emotional weight. The size gap between them is intentional; the label is small enough to feel like metadata.

The gold rule: 64px wide, 2px tall. It's the only decorative element, and its restraint mirrors the show's approach to humor: dry delivery, sharp punchline. The rule is the visual equivalent of a beat before the next scene.

Why centered: Centering signals authority. Left-aligned would feel like a slide deck. Centered with generous vertical space above and below reads as a title card in a documentary or a chapter page in a book.

Strengths

  • Maximum readability and clarity
  • Timeless. Won't look dated in 2 years
  • Dead simple to produce: Fusion text node or even a PNG
  • Consistent with existing style mockup direction
  • Works identically for short and long titles

Risks

  • Could feel too plain for YouTube, where thumbnails compete for attention
  • No visual variety chapter to chapter (same card, different words)
  • Relies entirely on font quality; any rendering issues are exposed
  • May not differentiate the series visually from other documentary channels

Direction B: Archival Cartographic

Military briefing meets antique atlas.

Left-aligned title against a faint grid field, with an oversized ghosted chapter number in the background. Corner registration marks and coordinate-style metadata give the card a sense of place, as though each chapter is a location on a map you're zooming into.

The logic: Civilization is fundamentally a game about maps and territory. This direction leans into that cartographic DNA without using any actual game imagery. The grid, the corner marks, and the coordinates create a "war room" atmosphere that fits chapters about military conflict (Ch 3, Ch 4) and also works for economic or diplomatic chapters because the framing is analytical, not aggressive.

Uses Cinzel, a typeface designed as a modern interpretation of Roman inscriptions. It carries more weight and gravitas than Lora, which shifts the cards from "editorial" to "monumental."

Design Notes

Asymmetric layout: Title left, ghost number right. The asymmetry creates visual tension and makes the card feel composed rather than generated. Left alignment also gives the title room to breathe on longer names without centering compression.

The ghost number: Oversized, stroked, barely visible. It gives the eye something to find, a layer of depth that rewards a second look. At 180px with a 1px stroke at 12% opacity, it reads as texture more than text.

Corner marks: Registration marks from print production. They frame the composition and subtly say "this was designed with intention." The effect is more felt than noticed.

Coordinate line: "CH 01 · GAME 1 · ANTIQUITY" reads like metadata on a military document. This is the one element that changes per chapter and adds information density without clutter.

Strengths

  • More visually distinctive than centered text; stands out on YouTube
  • The map/grid motif connects to Civ's core identity without showing the game
  • Ghost numbers add subtle variety chapter to chapter
  • Cinzel carries more gravitas than Lora; feels monumental
  • The coordinate metadata line can carry useful context

Risks

  • More complex to produce; needs a proper Fusion comp or After Effects
  • Cinzel introduces a third typeface (alongside Lora + Poppins in other overlays)
  • The cartographic framing could feel heavy-handed over 12 chapters
  • Grid and corner marks might read as "UI design mockup" rather than documentary

Direction C: Cinematic Field

The last frame before the lights come up.

Title positioned in the lower third of the frame, left-aligned, in italic serif. Cinematic letterbox bars top and bottom. Film grain texture. The card looks like the title card of an indie documentary or a prestige TV episode title.

The logic: this direction borrows the visual language of cinema rather than print or cartography. The letterbox bars signal "this is a film, not a slideshow." The italic EB Garamond carries elegance and movement (italic literally means "leaning forward"). Positioning the title low and left creates a composition that feels like a camera has settled on a scene, with the title appearing in the empty space.

The warm glow behind the text area and the grain texture add atmosphere without adding information. This is the most emotionally evocative of the three directions, and the one that takes the biggest risk by treating a Civ video like art cinema.

Design Notes

Lower-third positioning: Most title cards center their text. Putting it bottom-left creates a composition that feels like a documentary title superimposed over a scene. Even though there's no scene here (just darkness), the positioning carries that implication. It also visually rhymes with the lower third overlays used for leader names.

Italic serif: EB Garamond Italic is one of the most beautiful screen typefaces available. The italic form adds movement and elegance that upright text can't match. "Nothing Lasts Forever" in this typeface is going to hit differently than in Lora SemiBold.

Letterbox bars: 2.35:1 aspect ratio framing within the 16:9 frame. This is a pure cinema signifier. It says "what you're watching is crafted, not captured." The bars also compress the vertical space in a way that makes the composition feel more intentional.

Film grain: A subtle SVG noise texture at 4% opacity. It's felt, not seen. It adds organic texture to what would otherwise be a digital void, and it connects to the documentary film tradition the show references.

Strengths

  • The most emotionally resonant of the three; "Nothing Lasts Forever" will land hard
  • Letterbox bars are an instant visual differentiator from other gaming channels
  • Film grain adds warmth and texture; feels handmade
  • EB Garamond Italic is genuinely beautiful at display sizes
  • Low-left positioning creates sophisticated compositional tension

Risks

  • Could feel pretentious for a Civ video; "are we taking this too seriously?"
  • Italic titles are harder to read at small sizes (mobile viewers)
  • EB Garamond is a different typeface family from Lora; type system grows to 3+
  • The cinematic framing might set expectations the gameplay footage can't match
